Phase 1 — Foundation (Weeks 1–4)

Your only job this phase is to build physical habits. Fingers hurt, chords buzz, switching is slow — all of this is normal and temporary. Every professional guitarist went through exactly this. By week 4 your hands will start doing things automatically that feel impossible today.

Weeks 1–4 15–20 min/day Acoustic guitar 5–6 days/week
Week 1 — Your very first session
D1
Setup + Em chord
Posture, tuning, your first chord shape
20 min ▼
Before you touch the strings: Today is not about sounding good. It's about setting up the habits that will carry you through 20 weeks of learning. Take your time on every step. Read each instruction before doing it.
1
Tune your guitar 3 min
Download GuitarTuna (free). Open the app and pluck each string one at a time — low E, A, D, G, B, high E. The app will show you if it's too high or too low. Tighten the tuning peg to raise pitch, loosen to lower. Never skip tuning. Playing out of tune teaches your ears the wrong thing from day one.
2
Set your posture 2 min
Sit up straight in an armless chair. Rest the guitar's waist on your right thigh (if right-handed). The neck should angle slightly upward — not horizontal. Your fretting hand thumb sits behind the neck, roughly behind your middle finger. Your wrist should have a slight forward curve, not collapsed flat against the neck.
3
Learn the Em chord shape 7 min
Em uses only two fingers. Place your middle finger on string 5 (the second thickest), 2nd fret. Place your ring finger on string 4 (next to it), 2nd fret. Press firmly with your fingertips — not the pads. Your other fingers should not touch the strings. Strum all 6 strings slowly with your thumb. Every string should ring. If any buzz, check that your fingers are right behind the fret (not on it) and pressing firmly.
4
Pluck each string individually 3 min
With Em fretted, pluck string 6, then 5, 4, 3, 2, 1 one at a time. Each should ring clearly. If string 3, 2, or 1 buzzes, one of your fingers is accidentally touching it. Curve your fingers more so only the very tips touch the strings. Do this until all 6 ring clearly.
5
First strumming — down only 5 min
Hold the pick between your thumb and index finger — about half the pick showing. Strum downward across all 6 strings slowly. Count out loud: "1 — 2 — 3 — 4" with one strum per beat. Don't worry about rhythm yet. Just hear that you're making a chord sound. End the session here.
Record a 30-second video of yourself playing Em right now. Save it. You'll watch it again at the end of week 4 and the difference will be the clearest proof of progress you'll ever see.
End of session checklist
Guitar is in tune
Em chord fretted with middle + ring fingers on strings 5 and 4, 2nd fret
All 6 strings ring clearly when plucked individually
Day 1 video saved to phone
▶ Em chord tutorial (Justin Guitar)
D2
Am chord + first chord switch
Learn Am and begin switching between Em and Am
20 min ▼
Your fingers will be sore. This is callus formation — your fingertips are toughening up. It peaks around day 4–5 and gets dramatically better after week 2. Keep sessions short and push through it. Today you add Am and make your first chord switch.
1
Tune + warm up with Em 4 min
Tune first. Then fret Em and strum 8 times slowly. Count "1-2-3-4" for each group of 4. Your fingers need to warm up into the shape before you move on.
2
Learn the Am chord shape 5 min
Am uses three fingers. Index finger on string 2 (second thinnest), 1st fret. Middle finger on string 4, 2nd fret. Ring finger on string 3, 2nd fret. Strum strings 5 down to 1 — do NOT strum the 6th (thickest) string. Pluck each string individually and fix any buzzes before moving on.
3
The chord switch drill 8 min
This is the most important drill in Phase 1. Strum Em 4 times (counting 1-2-3-4). Then lift all fingers, form Am, and strum 4 times. Then back to Em. Do this continuously for 8 minutes. The switch will feel impossibly slow at first. That's fine. Speed comes from repetition, not from trying to go faster.
4
The 1-minute chord change test — Day 1 baseline 3 min
Set a 1-minute timer. Switch between Em and Am as many times as you can while keeping both chords clean. Count each full switch (Em to Am = 1). Write down your number. This is your baseline score — you'll beat it every single week.
Am and Em are the core of almost every Hozier song. The chord progression Am → G → C → F that appears in Work Song, Someone New, and From Eden is built on exactly these two chords. You are already playing the DNA of your favorite music.
End of session checklist
Am chord rings cleanly on strings 5–1
1-minute baseline score written down
Can switch Em → Am without stopping to think about finger placement
▶ Em and Am chord tutorial
D3
Music theory — the musical alphabet
Notes, frets, and what a chord actually is
20 min ▼
Theory days are active, not passive. You'll play along with everything you learn today. Theory without a guitar in your hands is just memorization. Theory with a guitar in your hands is understanding.
1
Warm up — chord switch drill 4 min
Tune your guitar. Do 4 minutes of Em → Am switches. You should already feel slightly faster than yesterday. Don't count — just flow.
2
The musical alphabet 5 min
There are only 7 natural notes in Western music: A B C D E F G. After G, it goes back to A and repeats. Between most notes there are sharps (#) or flats (b) — A, A#, B, C, C#, D, D#, E, F, F#, G, G#, then back to A. That's 12 notes total, repeating forever. Say this out loud three times: "A A# B C C# D D# E F F# G G#." This is the entire vocabulary of Western music.
3
Notes on the guitar 5 min
Each fret moves the note up by one step (one semitone). Your open strings (unfretted) are: E A D G B E from thick to thin. Play the open low E string. Now press fret 1 — that's F. Fret 2 is F#. Fret 3 is G. Fret 4 is G#. Fret 5 is A (same pitch as your open A string). Play each one slowly and listen to the pitch rising.
4
What a chord is 6 min
A chord is three or more notes played together. Em contains E, G, and B. Fret Em and pluck each string — strings 6 (E), 5 (B), 4 (E), 3 (G), 2 (B), 1 (E). You're hearing E, G, and B notes stacked together. Am contains A, C, and E. These three-note groups (called triads) are the building blocks of all the music you love.
Knowing the notes on your guitar isn't urgent right now — but understanding that every fret is a half-step helps you later when you learn scales, keys, and why certain chords sound right together. Plant the seed today, harvest it in Phase 2.
▶ Music theory for guitar beginners
D4
Strumming with a metronome
Building rhythm with Em and Am
20 min ▼
Rhythm is a separate skill from chord shapes. Today you deliberately practice playing in time. A metronome is your most important teacher for this — it doesn't lie, it doesn't slow down for you, and it shows you exactly when you're rushing or dragging.
1
Download Metronome Beats (free) and set to 60 BPM 2 min
60 BPM means 60 beats per minute — one beat per second. This feels slow. That is deliberate. You are training accuracy, not speed.
2
Strum Em in time 5 min
Fret Em. On every click of the metronome, strum down once. Four clicks = four strums. Count out loud: "1 — 2 — 3 — 4." The strum must happen exactly on the click, not before or after. If you miss a beat, don't try to catch up — just find the next click and land on it.
3
Switch to Am on beat 1 8 min
Strum Em four times (four clicks). On the next beat 1, switch to Am and strum four times. Switch back to Em on the next beat 1. The switch must happen on the beat — not after you've formed the chord. This means you start moving your fingers on beat 4, so your hand arrives in position on beat 1. This is a fundamental technique called "preparing the chord."
4
1-minute chord change test — week 1 check 3 min
Set 1-minute timer. Switch Em → Am as many times as possible with clean chords. Write down the number. Compare to Day 2's baseline.
5
Cool down — play freely 2 min
Put the metronome away. Switch between Em and Am however you like, at whatever speed feels comfortable. End every session doing something that feels good.
If you find yourself lifting your strumming hand to "wait" for the chord switch, you're moving too fast. Drop the metronome to 50 BPM. There is no shame in 50 BPM. Every professional guitarist practiced at 50 BPM.
▶ Beginner strumming fundamentals
D5
Consolidation + first song preview
Solidify Em and Am — preview Work Song by Hozier
20 min ▼
Today is about making what you know feel solid. No new chords. Just Em and Am with more intention — and your first glimpse of Work Song by Hozier, which you'll be able to play in full by week 4.
1
Tune and check your chord quality 4 min
Tune the guitar. Fret Em and pluck every string individually — all 6 must ring clearly. Then fret Am and pluck strings 5 through 1 — all 5 must ring clearly. Fix any buzzes before continuing. This individual-string check is how you confirm your chord is actually correct.
2
Metronome switching at 65 BPM 7 min
Slightly faster than yesterday. Em for 4 beats, Am for 4 beats. Switch on beat 1. Keep counting out loud. If 65 BPM feels too fast for clean switches, go back to 60 and stay there — there is no rush.
3
Listen to Work Song by Hozier 4 min
Put your guitar down and just listen to Work Song. Notice the strumming rhythm — it's steady and repetitive. Notice when the chord changes happen. The verse uses Am and G almost exclusively. You already know Am. You are closer to playing this song than you think.
4
Play Am along with the song 5 min
Play the song again. Just strum Am on every beat — don't worry about switching to other chords yet. Just feel what it's like to have your guitar making sound alongside music you love. This is why you're learning guitar.
Playing along with recordings is one of the best training tools that exists. Your ear starts learning the rhythm, the feel, and the timing of real music rather than just a metronome click. Do this with every song you love, even when you can only play one chord of it.
D6
Rest day
Active recovery — listening practice
Optional ▼
Rest is part of the plan
Your fingers need a break to form calluses and your brain needs time to consolidate what you've practiced. If you want to do something music-related today, listen actively to your favorite artists. Pick one song and try to count the beats, notice when chords change, and identify the strumming rhythm. This builds your ear without straining your fingers.
Week 2 — Adding G and C chords
D7
G chord introduction
Learn G and add it to your chord vocabulary
20 min ▼
G is the most important chord in folk and indie music. Hozier, Bon Iver, Gregory Alan Isakov, and Mumford & Sons all center enormous amounts of their music in the key of G. Learning it today unlocks a huge amount of your repertoire.
1
Tune + Em/Am warm-up at 65 BPM 4 min
Two minutes of Em → Am switching with the metronome. Notice how much easier this already feels compared to Day 1.
2
Learn the G chord 6 min
G uses three fingers in the corners of the guitar. Middle finger on string 6 (thickest), 3rd fret. Index finger on string 5, 2nd fret. Ring finger on string 1 (thinnest), 3rd fret. (Some people use pinky on string 1 instead of ring — either is fine, but the pinky version prepares you better for chord transitions.) Strum all 6 strings. All 6 should ring. The stretch feels uncomfortable at first — this is normal.
3
G → Em switching drill 8 min
G and Em share the middle finger on string 5. Use this as an anchor — keep that finger on string 5, 2nd fret and only move your other fingers. Strum G four times, switch to Em four times, repeat. The shared finger trick makes this transition faster than you'd expect.
4
Play G → Em → Am continuously 2 min
Three chords in a row. G (4 strums) → Em (4 strums) → Am (4 strums) → back to G. This is the basis of hundreds of folk and indie songs. It will feel messy — that's where you are right now, and that's exactly where you should be.
▶ G chord lesson
D8
C chord introduction
Learn C and practice the G–C–Am–Em progression
20 min ▼
C is the second-most important chord you'll learn. Together, G, C, Am, and Em form the I-VI-IV-V progression that appears in an enormous percentage of folk, indie, and alternative songs. Once you can switch between all four, you can approximate hundreds of songs.
1
Tune + G chord warm-up 3 min
Form G and strum 8 times, counting in groups of four. Then switch to Em 8 times. Warm the shapes up before adding anything new.
2
Learn the C chord 6 min
C uses three fingers in a diagonal line. Ring finger on string 5, 3rd fret. Middle finger on string 4, 2nd fret. Index finger on string 2, 1st fret. Strum strings 5 through 1 — do NOT strum the 6th string. The index finger position (string 2, 1st fret) is the tricky one — make sure it doesn't accidentally mute string 1. Pluck each string to check.
3
The G → C → Am → Em progression 10 min
This is the backbone of Work Song, and dozens of other songs in your taste. Practice it: G (4 strums) → C (4 strums) → Am (4 strums) → Em (4 strums). Repeat continuously. Look for shared fingers between chords — C and Am share the ring and middle fingers almost in the same position. This makes that switch faster.
4
Identify the progression in Work Song 1 min
Put Work Song on. Listen for the G → C → Am → Em pattern in the verse. You're hearing the exact chords you just practiced. Your brain is already connecting the sounds to the shapes.
▶ 4-chord progression practice
D9
Music theory — what is a key?
Why G, C, Am, and Em always sound right together
20 min ▼
Today you learn WHY the chords you've been playing sound right together. This is the single most useful piece of music theory for a guitarist — and once it clicks, you'll start hearing the structure of every song you love.
1
Warm up — four-chord progression 4 min
G → C → Am → Em, four strums each, twice through. Make it as clean as you can.
2
What is a key? 6 min
A key is a family of notes that sound good together. The key of G major contains exactly these notes: G A B C D E F#. All the chords built from these notes belong in the key of G. G major (I), Am (II), Bm (III), C major (IV), D major (V), Em (VI), F#dim (VII). Notice: G, C, Am, and Em are all on this list. That's why they sound right together — they're all in the same family.
3
Play the I–IV–V–I progression 6 min
In G major: I = G, IV = C, V = D (you'll learn D next week), VI = Em. Play G → C → G → Em repeatedly. Listen to how G feels like "home" — it's the tonic, the place the music wants to return to. This feeling of resolution is the I chord doing its job.
4
Identify the key of Work Song 4 min
Work Song by Hozier is in the key of Am (A minor). Its main chords are Am, G, C, and F. All four are in the key of A minor. Listen to the song and notice how Am feels like "home" — the place the music resolves to. This is the tonic minor chord doing the same job G does in a major key.
Once you know a song's key, you immediately know which chords are likely to appear in it. This is how professional musicians learn songs quickly — they identify the key first, then narrow down the possibilities dramatically.
▶ Keys and scales explained simply
D10
Down-up strumming pattern
Adding upstrokes — the sound of folk guitar
20 min ▼
You've been strumming down only — that changes today. Adding upstrokes creates the rhythmic feel of acoustic folk music. The key insight: on downstrokes, strum all strings. On upstrokes, only catch the top 3 or 4 strings. This gives folk music its characteristic sound.
1
Tune + warm up all four chords 4 min
G → C → Am → Em, two strums each. Go through it twice. Just warming up.
2
Add the upstroke — pattern: D DU 6 min
Stay on Em. Pattern: Down — Down — Up. Count: "1 — 2 — and." The "and" is the upstroke, happening between beats 2 and 3. The upstroke should be a light, relaxed flick — don't try to catch every string. Practice this continuously on Em for 6 minutes at 60 BPM before adding chord changes.
3
Apply D DU to the four-chord progression 8 min
G (D DU) → C (D DU) → Am (D DU) → Em (D DU). Switch every 3 strums. This will feel awkward — you're coordinating two hands doing different things while also switching chords. Slow it down. Start at 50 BPM if needed.
4
Play the progression and feel the folk rhythm 2 min
Without a metronome. Just play G → C → Am → Em with the D DU pattern at a comfortable pace. Listen to how it sounds like actual music now, not just individual chords.
D11
First song attempt — Work Song (Hozier)
Play your first complete song from start to finish
20 min ▼
Today you play a real song for the first time. It will not be perfect. It will be slow, there will be buzzes, and you'll lose the chord changes. That's fine. The goal today is just to play through Work Song from beginning to end without stopping, even if it sounds rough.
1
Watch the tutorial first 5 min
Watch the Work Song beginner tutorial all the way through before you play a note. Understand the chord order, where the changes happen, and what the strumming pattern is. Then close it and try it yourself.
2
Practice just the verse chords 7 min
The verse is Am → G → C → G. That's it. Practice switching those four chords in order, two strums each, until the sequence feels somewhat automatic. Don't move to the full song until this loop feels manageable.
3
Play through the whole song slowly 6 min
Put the tutorial on and play along — but at half the original speed if needed. When you lose a chord change, don't stop. Skip the chord, keep strumming, and catch the next change. Never stop mid-song to fix a mistake. Train yourself to keep going.
4
Record yourself playing it 2 min
Record one full play-through. Don't listen to it yet — save it for the end of Phase 1 to compare with your Week 4 version.
The number one rule for playing songs: never stop. When you stop to fix a mistake mid-song, you're training yourself to stop mid-song. Real music never stops. Keep the rhythm going and let bad notes pass.

Week 3 — D chord, the full strumming pattern, and Work Song
D13
D chord + the folk strumming pattern
Learn D and master D-DU-UDU
20 min ▼
D is the last of your essential open chords. And today you learn the strumming pattern D-DU-UDU — the most common pattern in folk and acoustic rock. Once you have these two things together, you can play a huge number of complete songs.
1
Tune + warm up 3 min
G → C → Am → Em, D DU pattern, two full loops. Notice how much smoother this is compared to week 1.
2
Learn the D chord 5 min
D is a triangle shape on the top four strings. Index finger on string 3 (G string), 2nd fret. Ring finger on string 2 (B string), 3rd fret. Middle finger on string 1 (high E), 2nd fret. Strum strings 4 through 1 only — never strum the 5th or 6th string on a D chord. The fingering is tight and the triangle shape feels cramped. This eases with practice.
3
Learn the D-DU-UDU strumming pattern 7 min
This is the pattern: Down — Down-Up — Up-Down-Up. Count it: "1 — 2-and — and-3-and." The key is keeping your strumming hand moving constantly like a pendulum — down on downbeats, up on upbeats. Even when you're not hitting the strings, your hand keeps moving in rhythm. Practice on G for the full 7 minutes before adding chord changes.
4
Apply to G → D → Em → C 5 min
This progression + this strumming pattern = the feel of dozens of folk songs. Keep your strumming hand moving even when your fretting hand is switching. The rhythm never stops.

D14
Work Song — deep practice
Learn verse, chorus, and bridge separately
25 min ▼
Today you stop just surviving the song and start actually learning it. Professional musicians don't practice songs from start to finish repeatedly — they isolate the hard sections and drill them until they're easier than the easy sections. Today you do the same with Work Song.
1
Identify the hardest chord switch in the song 3 min
Play through Work Song once. Find the one chord transition that causes you to lose the rhythm every time. That's your target for today. Common culprits: the G → C switch or the C → Am switch.
2
Drill that single transition for 10 minutes 10 min
Just those two chords. Four strums each, metronome at 60 BPM. Nothing else. Ten minutes of this one switch. This is how songs get learned — not by playing them over and over, but by identifying the weak point and attacking it directly.
3
Play through the full song twice 10 min
Now play Work Song twice in a row without stopping. Notice whether that drilled transition is cleaner. It will be. Ending with the full song consolidates the drilling.
4
1-minute chord change test — week 3 check 2 min
Compare to your baseline from Day 2. Write it down.
D15
Theory — major vs minor and why it matters
The emotional difference between major and minor chords
20 min ▼
Major chords sound bright. Minor chords sound dark and emotional. This is the single most important distinction in music — and it directly explains why Hozier's music sounds the way it does. Almost everything you love is built on the tension between these two qualities.
1
Feel the difference — G vs Am 3 min
Strum G four times and listen — bright, open, resolved. Then strum Am four times and listen — darker, more emotional, slightly tense. Switch between them slowly and really hear the difference. Major = happy/bright. Minor = dark/emotional. This isn't a rule — it's a physical reality of the intervals between the notes.
2
Why minor chords sound dark 5 min
A minor chord has a "flattened third" — the middle note of the three-note chord is lowered by one fret compared to a major chord. Am contains A, C, E. A major contains A, C#, E. That one note (C vs C#) is what creates the entire emotional difference. Play Am, then imagine raising your middle finger from fret 2 to fret 3 — that would give you A major. Try it. Hear the shift from dark to bright with a single finger movement.
3
Find major and minor in your favorite songs 7 min
Listen to Work Song. The verse stays mostly on Am and G — minor-led. The chorus lifts to C and G — more major. This is Hozier moving between darkness and light with chord choices. Listen for this in everything you play from now on.
4
Play the Am → C progression repeatedly 5 min
Am (minor, dark) → C (major, bright). Just these two. Feel the emotional arc: tension → release, dark → light. This two-chord movement is at the heart of so much folk and indie music. Gregory Alan Isakov, Bon Iver, and Hozier use it constantly.
D16
Free play + consolidation
Play what you love, explore, have fun
20 min ▼
No structured exercises today. Free play sessions are as important as drills. Today you remind yourself why you're doing this.
1
Play along with your playlist 15 min
Put on your favorite songs. Play whatever chords you know along with them. When you don't know the chord, just strum the one that sounds most right. Don't pause the song. Don't look anything up. Just play and listen and feel the music.
2
Play Work Song for someone 5 min
Or record it and send it to someone. Playing for another person — even if it's imperfect — activates a different kind of attention and motivation than practicing alone. It's one of the most powerful tools for staying consistent.

Week 4 — Fmaj7, fingerpicking intro, and phase graduation
D18
Fmaj7 — the easy F chord
A beautiful, beginner-friendly F variation
20 min ▼
The full F barre chord is Phase 2 material. But Fmaj7 is a gorgeous, simpler version of F that appears in hundreds of folk songs and sounds just as beautiful — sometimes more so. It's used by Hozier, Gregory Alan Isakov, and John Mayer constantly.
1
Tune + full four-chord warm-up 4 min
G → C → Am → Em with the D-DU-UDU pattern. Two full loops. Feel how much easier this is than week 1.
2
Learn Fmaj7 5 min
Fmaj7 uses just two fingers. Index finger bars strings 1 AND 2 at fret 1 (press both down with the flat of your index finger). Middle finger on string 3, 2nd fret. Strum strings 4 through 1. Leave strings 5 and 6 out. This chord sounds lush and open — very different from the harsh barre F. Many professional guitarists prefer it.
3
Am → Fmaj7 → C → G progression 8 min
This is the i-VI-III-VII progression in A minor and it's everywhere in Hozier. Practice it with four strums per chord until the switches feel somewhat smooth. The Am → Fmaj7 switch is the key one to drill — find any shared finger positions and use them as anchors.
4
Listen for Fmaj7 in songs you know 3 min
Play Something In The Way She Moves by James Taylor or Work Song. Notice the warm, slightly unresolved quality of that Fmaj7 chord sound. You'll start hearing it everywhere now that you know it.
D19
Intro to fingerpicking
Your first fingerpicking pattern on Em and Am
20 min ▼
Fingerpicking is the sound of Bon Iver, Gregory Alan Isakov, and acoustic John Mayer. Today is just an introduction — you'll develop it fully in Phase 2. But planting the seed now means it won't feel entirely foreign when you return to it.
1
Set your right hand position 3 min
Rest your thumb on string 6 (or 5). Your index (i) sits over string 3, middle (m) over string 2, ring (a) over string 1. These are your default positions. Your hand should float just above the strings without resting on the guitar body.
2
The basic pattern: p-i-m-a 8 min
On Em, fretted: Thumb plucks string 6 (low E), then index plucks string 3, then middle plucks string 2, then ring plucks string 1. p-i-m-a. One finger at a time, slowly. Each note should ring clearly and not be muted by other fingers. Repeat this four-note pattern continuously for 8 minutes. Don't rush it.
3
Apply to Am 5 min
Same pattern on Am. Thumb on string 5 (A string) instead of 6. p-i-m-a. Then alternate: 4 times on Em, 4 times on Am. The switch will disrupt your picking hand — keep the pattern going and let your fretting hand make the chord change underneath it.
4
Listen to Skinny Love by Bon Iver 2 min
This is your Phase 2 song target. Listen to the fingerpicking pattern. You just learned the building blocks of that sound today. In 4 weeks you'll be able to play it.

Week 5 — F chord and fingerpicking development
D21
Full F barre chord — introduction
The hardest chord you'll learn, approached correctly
30 min ▼
The F barre chord stops most beginners. Here's why it doesn't have to stop you. The secret is that you don't need to play it cleanly from day one. You need to practice it a little every day for several weeks. The strength and flexibility build gradually. Don't judge your F chord by how it sounds today — judge it by how it sounds in week 8.
1
Tune + Phase 1 warm-up 5 min
G → C → Am → Em with strumming pattern. Two loops. Remind your fingers they know how to do this.
2
The Fmaj7 as your bridge to F 3 min
Play Fmaj7 and get comfortable with your index finger pressing strings 1 and 2 at fret 1. This index finger position is exactly what you'll use in the full F barre chord — you're extending it to cover all 6 strings.
3
Attempt the full F barre chord 10 min
Lay your index finger flat across all 6 strings at fret 1. Press firmly — your index finger needs to be as close to the 1st fret as possible without being on it. Now add: ring finger on string 5, 3rd fret. Pinky on string 4, 3rd fret. Middle finger on string 3, 2nd fret. Pluck each string individually. Most will buzz. That's normal. D23
Theory — the major scale and how chords are built
Understanding scales gives you the map of the fretboard
25 min ▼
The major scale is the DNA of Western music. Every chord, every key, every song you love is built from it. Understanding it takes 10 minutes. Using it fluently takes years. Today you understand it — fluency comes naturally over time.
1
Warm up — Travis picking on Em and Am 5 min
Five minutes of Travis picking, switching between Em and Am. Keep your thumb alternating.
2
Play the G major scale on one string 8 min
On string 6 (low E): Open = E. Fret 3 = G. The G major scale starting from G: G(3) A(5) B(7) C(8) D(10) E(12) F#(14) G(15). Play each fret slowly, saying the note name out loud. Then play it backward. The pattern of gaps (W W H W W W H, where W=2 frets and H=1 fret) is the same for every major scale — only the starting position changes.
3
How chords are built from the scale 7 min
Take notes 1, 3, and 5 of the G major scale: G, B, D. That's a G major chord. Take notes 6, 1, and 3 of G major: E, G, B. That's an Em chord. Every chord in the key of G is built by stacking scale notes. This is why the key of G "contains" specific chords — because its scale contains specific notes.
4
F barre chord practice 5 min
Five minutes of F barre chord attempts. Is it getting any cleaner? Even marginally? That's progress.

Theory — the Nashville Number System
Learn any song faster by thinking in numbers, not chord names
25 min ▼
The Nashville Number System is how professional session musicians communicate instantly in any key. Instead of saying "G, C, Am, Em," they say "1, 4, 6m, 3m." Once you understand this, you can transpose any song to any key instantly — and you understand why chord progressions work regardless of what key they're in.
1
Number the chords in G major 5 min
In the key of G: G=1, Am=2m, Bm=3m, C=4, D=5, Em=6m, F#dim=7dim. Work Song's verse (Am G C G) in the key of Am is: 1m, VII, III, VII. Play the Work Song progression and say the numbers out loud instead of chord names.
2
The most common progressions — decoded 8 min
1-4-5-1 (G-C-D-G): The foundation of blues and country. 1-5-6m-4 (G-D-Em-C): The "Axis of Awesome" progression — Let Her Go, Can't Help Falling in Love, hundreds more. 1-6m-4-5 (G-Em-C-D): Classic folk. Play each one and listen for songs you recognize.
3
Apply to a Hozier song 7 min
From Eden by Hozier is mostly 1-4-1-5 in Dm. In the key of D minor: Dm(1) Gm(4) Dm(1) A(5). Understanding the number system lets you identify that Hozier uses the same fundamental progressions as hundreds of folk songs — just with different chord textures and voicings layered on top.

D51
E-shape barre chord — moveable major chord
One shape, every major chord on the neck
40 min ▼
The E-shape barre chord is the key to the entire fretboard. You know how to play E major open. Barre your index across all strings and slide the whole shape up the neck — every position gives you a new major chord. G major at fret 3. A major at fret 5. B major at fret 7. The same one shape gives you every major chord that exists.
1
Tune + full warm-up 7 min
G → C → Am → Em with strumming pattern. Then Travis picking on Em and Am. Then F barre chord attempts. Full warm-up before attempting new barre work.
2
E-shape barre at fret 5 (A major) 12 min
Index bars all 6 strings at fret 5. Ring finger on string 5, fret 7. Pinky on string 4, fret 7. Middle finger on string 3, fret 6. (This is the same layout as F major but moved up 4 frets.) This is A major. Pluck each string. Work on any buzzing strings. Your F chord practice has been preparing your index for exactly this. The higher up the neck you play, the easier barre chords feel — so fret 5 is actually easier than fret 1 for the F chord.
3
Slide the shape to G (fret 3) and B (fret 7) 10 min
Move the exact same shape to fret 3 — G major barre chord. Then fret 7 — B major. Strum each and fix buzzes. Then practice: G (fret 3) → A (fret 5) → B (fret 7) in sequence. Notice how the same shape gives you three different chords just by moving your hand position.
4
E-shape minor barre chord 8 min
Same barre position, but base the shape on Em instead of E major. Remove the middle finger from string 3 — that's it. At fret 5: Am barre. At fret 7: Bm barre. At fret 2: F#m. These minor barre chords open up keys that are very difficult without them — including John Mayer's key of C#m in Slow Dancing in a Burning Room.
5
F barre chord at fret 1 — direct practice 3 min
After warming up the barre chord at easier positions, return to fret 1 for the F chord. It should ring more clearly now that your index finger has been working at higher frets.

Capo mastery — playing in any key with open shapes
How Hozier and Mumford & Sons use the capo
35 min ▼
The capo is not a crutch — it's a creative tool. Professional guitarists use capos constantly because they change the timbre and register of the guitar, not just the key. Hozier's Take Me to Church with a capo on fret 1 sounds different from playing the same chord shapes without the capo, even though the notes are only one semitone apart. Understanding this gives you control over your sound.
1
Warm up — barre chord work from Day 51 8 min

2
Understand capo transposition 5 min
Capo 1 = everything sounds one semitone higher. Capo 2 = one whole step higher. Capo 5 = everything sounds a perfect 4th higher. If you play a G chord shape with capo 5, you're playing a C chord sound. If you play Am shape with capo 1, you're playing A#m (Bbm). Write this down: Capo position + open chord shape = actual chord sound. Google "capo chord transposition chart" for a reference.

Place capo on fret 1. The verse uses Am-shape (sounds Bbm), G-shape (sounds Ab), C-shape (sounds Db), F-shape (sounds Gb/F). Practice the verse chord progression slowly with the capo on. Notice how familiar the shapes feel even though the sound is completely different from what you'd expect.
4
Learn The Cave setup — capo 5 10 min
Remove capo from fret 1, place on fret 5. The Cave uses G, D, Em, C shapes — all chords you know in their open positions. Practice the driving strum: all downstrokes at first, then add the muted upstroke technique. Hold your strumming hand loosely against the strings mid-strum for the percussive "chunk" sound.
5

Capo 5. Play the chords along with the Mumford & Sons recording. Feel the driving momentum of that strumming. You're playing a song you've heard thousands of times.

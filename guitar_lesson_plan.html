Phase 1 — Foundation (weeks 1–4)

The goal is not to sound good yet. The goal is to build the physical habits — finger placement, posture, and switching between two chords — so that by week 4 your hands start doing things automatically. This is where most people quit. Don't.

Weeks 1–4 15–20 min/day Acoustic guitar 5–6 days/week
Setup — before you play a single note

Which guitar to start on

Use your acoustic for Phase 1. It builds finger strength faster. Electric feels easier but won't develop calluses or strength as efficiently.

Get in tune every session

Download GuitarTuna (free). Always tune before playing. Playing out of tune trains your ear incorrectly from day one.

Posture

Sit up straight. Guitar rests on your right leg (if right-handed). Fret hand thumb behind the neck, not over the top.

Fingertips will hurt

This is normal and lasts 2–3 weeks. Play through it — calluses are forming. Shorter daily sessions hurt less than long ones.

Week 1–2 — Your first chords

Learn Em and Am first (not G, C, D)

Most beginner plans start with G, C, and D. These are harder. Em (two fingers) and Am (three fingers) are simpler shapes and actually appear in nearly every song you like. Starting here means you play real music faster.

  • Em: Middle and ring finger on strings 5 and 4, 2nd fret. Strum all 6 strings.
  • Am: Index on string 2, middle on string 4, ring on string 3 — all 2nd fret. Strum strings 5 to 1.
  • Practice switching between Em and Am for 5 minutes daily. Count "1-2-3-4" and switch on beat 1.
▶ Em and Am chord tutorial (Justin Guitar)

Strumming — down strums only to start

For the first two weeks, only strum downward. One strum per beat. Four beats per chord. This sounds boring but it forces your fretting hand to be clean before your strumming hand gets complicated.

  • Count out loud: "1 — 2 — 3 — 4" as you strum
  • Every note in the chord should ring clearly. If one buzzes, stop and adjust your finger position
  • Use a metronome app (Metronome Beats — free) at 60 BPM to start

Week 3–4 — Add G and C

G and C chords + your first chord progressions

By week 3 your fingers are stronger and Em/Am switching is more automatic. Now add G and C to unlock the most common progressions in folk and indie music.

  • G chord: Ring finger string 1 (3rd fret), middle finger string 5 (2nd fret), pinky string 6 (3rd fret)
  • C chord: Ring finger string 5 (3rd fret), middle finger string 4 (2nd fret), index finger string 2 (1st fret)
  • Practice the G → C → Am → Em progression — this is the backbone of most Hozier and Mumford songs

Music theory — week 1–4

The musical alphabet and what a chord actually is

You don't need to read sheet music. But understanding these three things will make everything else click faster.

  • The musical alphabet: A B C D E F G — then it repeats. That's all of Western music.
  • What a note is: Each fret on your guitar = one step (semitone). Moving up one fret raises the pitch by one semitone.
  • What a chord is: Three or more notes played together. Em contains the notes E, G, and B. That's it.
  • Why this matters for you: When you learn that "Hozier mostly plays in the key of G," you'll immediately know which chords are most likely to appear in his songs.

Phase 2 — Build (weeks 5–10)

You have four open chords. Now we expand your chord vocabulary, introduce fingerpicking — the defining sound of Bon Iver, Gregory Alan Isakov, and early John Mayer — and start understanding why songs sound the way they do.

Weeks 5–10 20–30 min/day Acoustic + electric 5 days/week
New chords — weeks 5–6

F chord (the wall every beginner hits) + D chord

The F chord requires a barre — pressing one finger across all strings. This is hard. Expect 2–3 weeks before it sounds clean. Do not skip it or use a shortcut shape permanently.

  • Start with Fmaj7: Index on strings 1 and 2 (1st fret), middle on string 3 (2nd fret). This is the "easy F" and sounds beautiful in folk music.
  • Full F barre chord: Index bars all strings at fret 1, ring on string 5 (3rd fret), pinky on string 4 (3rd fret), middle on string 3 (2nd fret).
  • D chord: Index on string 3 (2nd fret), middle on string 1 (2nd fret), ring on string 2 (3rd fret). Strum strings 4 to 1 only.

Fingerpicking — weeks 6–8

Travis picking pattern — the sound of your favorite artists

Fingerpicking is what makes Bon Iver, Gregory Alan Isakov, and John Mayer's acoustic work sound like that. It's not as hard as it looks, but it requires your thumb and fingers to become independent of each other.

  • Thumb (p): Plays the bass strings (4, 5, 6) — alternates between them
  • Index (i): Plays string 3
  • Middle (m): Plays string 2
  • Ring (a): Plays string 1
  • Basic pattern on Am: Thumb-5, index-3, middle-2, ring-1, thumb-4, index-3, middle-2, ring-1
  • Practice at 50 BPM until automatic, then increase 5 BPM per session

Electric guitar intro — weeks 8–10

Switching to electric + basic lead playing

Electric guitar opens up Role Model and John Mayer territory. The techniques are different — lighter touch, thinner strings, and now amp tone matters.

  • Lower action: Electric guitar strings are much easier to press. You'll immediately notice the difference.
  • Pentatonic scale — box 1: This is the most important scale in rock, blues, and indie. John Mayer plays 90% of his solos from this one shape.
  • Power chords: Two-finger chords used constantly in alternative rock. Root note + 5th. Move the same shape anywhere on the neck.
  • Basic bends: Push a string sideways to raise its pitch. This is the defining gesture of electric guitar expression.

Music theory — weeks 5–10

Keys, scales, and why chords belong together

This is the theory that explains why the G → C → Am → Em progression sounds so natural. It's because all four chords live in the same key.

  • The major scale: 7 notes with a specific pattern of gaps (W-W-H-W-W-W-H). Every key follows this pattern starting from a different note.
  • Chords in a key: In the key of G, the natural chords are G, Am, Bm, C, D, Em, F#dim. This is why those chords sound right together.
  • The I-IV-V-I progression: The most common in all of Western music. In G: G → C → D → G. You'll hear this in half your playlist.
  • Relative minor: Every major key has a relative minor that uses the same notes. G major's relative minor is Em. This is why G and Em always sound good together.

Phase 3 — Expand (weeks 11–20)

Barre chords, more advanced strumming, capo use, basic soloing, and the theory to understand any song by ear. By the end of Phase 3 you'll be able to look up a chord chart and play almost any song in your genre within a few practice sessions.

Weeks 11–20 30–45 min/day Both guitars 5 days/week
Barre chords — weeks 11–13

Full barre chords — the key to playing in any key

A barre chord is a moveable chord shape. Learn the shape once, slide it to any fret, and you have a new chord. This is what unlocks the entire fretboard.

  • E-shape barre chord: Based on the open E chord shape, barred at any fret. At fret 3 = G major. At fret 5 = A major.
  • A-shape barre chord: Based on the open A chord shape. At fret 3 = C major. At fret 5 = D major.
  • Minor versions: Same shapes using Em and Am as the base. These are equally important.
  • Barre chords will buzz for weeks. This is normal. Squeeze harder, place your index closer to the fret, and practice 5 minutes daily.

Capo use — weeks 12–14

The capo — why Mumford & Sons and Hozier live here

A capo clamps across the fretboard and raises the key of the guitar. It lets you use familiar open chord shapes in any key — which is exactly what Mumford & Sons, Hozier, and Gregory Alan Isakov do constantly.

  • Example: Mumford & Sons' "The Cave" is played with a capo on fret 5, using basic open chord shapes. Without the capo, the same song would require difficult barre chords in A major.
  • Hozier's "Take Me to Church": Capo 1, basic Em/Am shapes. Sounds huge, plays simply.
  • Buy a capo (~$10–15). It's one of the best investments a beginner can make for playing songs they actually love.

Advanced strumming — weeks 13–15

Strumming patterns — the rhythmic identity of folk and indie

  • Down-up strumming: D-DU-UDU — the most common pattern in folk and acoustic rock
  • Muted strums: Rest the palm on the strings mid-strum for a percussive "chunk" — essential for Mumford & Sons
  • Accent strumming: Hit beats 2 and 4 harder — gives music a driving folk feel
  • Always learn the rhythm of a song by listening first, tapping on your knee, then transferring it to the guitar
▶ Strumming patterns for folk and indie
Music theory — weeks 11–20

Chord extensions, the Nashville number system, and playing by ear

  • Chord extensions: Add7, sus2, sus4 chords — these are the "color" chords that make John Mayer and Gregory Alan Isakov's music feel atmospheric and rich
  • The Nashville Number System: Label chords by their position in the key (1, 2m, 3m, 4, 5, 6m) instead of by name. Lets you instantly transpose any song to any key.
  • Playing by ear: Start identifying whether a chord change sounds like it goes "up" or "down," and whether it feels resolved or unresolved. This builds the intuition to figure out songs without chord charts.
  • Intervals: The distance between two notes. Recognizing intervals by sound (a 4th sounds like "Here Comes the Bride," a 5th like "Star Wars") is the foundation of playing by ear.

Feedback and progress tools

The two reasons you've quit before — "progress feels slow" and "I can't tell if I'm playing correctly" — have specific solutions. Here they are.

Solving "I can't tell if I'm playing correctly"

Yousician Freemium

Listens through your phone mic and grades your playing in real time. Tells you which notes are wrong and which strings are buzzing. The closest thing to a teacher watching you.

GuitarTuna Free

Tune before every single session. Playing out of tune is the fastest way to develop bad ear habits. This takes 30 seconds and matters more than people think.

Record yourself Free

Your phone camera is your best feedback tool. Record each practice session from the side. Watch your fretting hand — if a chord buzzes, the video will show you exactly why.

Chord buzzing test Free

After fretting a chord, pluck each string individually. Any string that buzzes or mutes is a problem finger. Adjust until all 6 (or 5) strings ring clearly, then strum.

Solving "progress feels slow"

The 1-minute chord change test

Set a timer for 1 minute. Count how many clean chord changes you make (Em→Am, Em→Am...). Write it down. Do this weekly. Watching that number go from 8 to 24 to 40 is concrete, measurable proof of progress.

Video diary

Record yourself playing something on day 1 of each phase. Play it back at the end of the phase. The contrast is dramatic and will make you want to keep going.

Justin Guitar app Free

The gold standard free guitar learning app. Structured lessons, progress tracking, and a built-in practice timer. Used by millions of self-taught players.

Metronome Beats Free

Never practice without a metronome. Playing in time is a skill that needs training separately from chord shapes. Start every new thing at 60 BPM and only increase when it's clean.

How to structure each practice session

  • 5 min — Warm up: Slow chord changes, no pressure. Just waking up your fingers.
  • 5 min — Weak spot drill: Whatever was hardest last session. F chord, a tricky transition, a fingerpicking pattern.
  • 10 min — Song work: Current song from the roadmap. Work on one difficult section, not the whole song.
  • 5 min — Play something you already know: End every session on something that sounds good. This is crucial for motivation.
The most important rule: Always end your practice session playing something that sounds good to you — even if it's just one chord or a simple riff you learned in week 1. Ending on a win keeps you coming back.
